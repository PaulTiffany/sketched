# Philosophy

> This is the canonical narrative for Sketched — the *why* and the vision. For
> what is actually implemented today versus what is still vision, see
> [`docs/01_MVP0_CONTRACT.md`](docs/01_MVP0_CONTRACT.md). The `docs/` folder holds
> the buildable specs; this file is the north star they answer to.

## Sketched is Chalked through time

**Chalked** began with a simple surface: a shared board, a piece of chalk, an eraser, and rules for who may mark, erase, witness, or revise.

**Sketched** keeps that ethic, but moves the surface into time.

A chalk mark is a claim on a board.

A sketch layer is a claim across frames.

A knob is a bounded gesture through time.

An agent is a live knob.

A shake is revocation.

A stream is a living surface.

Sketched asks what happens when a shared whiteboard becomes a realtime scene: not only a place to draw, but a place where video, generated imagery, masks, annotations, controls, and live agent-knobs can coexist under human authority.

The goal is not to make the machine take over the scene.

The goal is to let the human hold the center while bounded generative processes build around, behind, beside, and sometimes with them.

## The toy is serious

Sketched deliberately uses the language of toys:

* sketch

* shake

* knob

* layer

* surface

* trace

* frame

This is not because the work is unserious.

It is because toys are among the first systems where humans learn constraint.

A good toy teaches by limiting possibility. It gives the hand a rule. It gives the eye a field. It makes cause and effect visible.

A chalkboard teaches inscription and erasure.

A mechanical drawing toy teaches parameterized motion.

A camera teaches presence.

A mask teaches boundary.

A timeline teaches memory.

A modular control surface teaches routing, signal, pressure, and coupling.

Sketched combines these into a small local system where generated scenes can be built, changed, shaken, and audited without pretending that the agent owns the world.

## The zero-point of coordination is human authority

Sketched does not begin with an agent.

It does not begin with a lease.

It does not begin with a director.

It begins with the human authority-bearing observer at the center of the shared surface.

The zero-point of coordination is human authority.

Everything else is routed relative to that point:

* layers

* masks

* streams

* knobs

* agents

* channels

* permissions

* directors

* audit logs

* shake operations

An agent-knob may generate continuously.

A channel may permit realtime flow.

A lease or permission record may define scope.

A director may route authority.

But none of these is the origin of the space.

They are coordination structures around the human-held center.

In the companion theory, this origin is the **void** — the unique shared depth-0 point of a surface, the localhost zero-point (see [`docs/12_FORCING_CORRESPONDENCE.md`](docs/12_FORCING_CORRESPONDENCE.md)). The theory keeps that point *neutral*: it is a shared origin, not a person. Sketched makes the normative move the theory declines — it **seats the human at that origin — and the instrument of that seating is the contract** ([`EULA.md`](EULA.md)). The math gives the shared origin; the EULA places the human there.

## Angular access

Authority in Sketched is not merely binary.

It is angular.

The human-held center is the zero-point of coordination. From that zero-point, access is granted along bounded angles into the shared surface.

An agent-knob does not receive the whole room.

It receives an angle.

An angle may include:

* what the agent can observe

* which stream or layer it can read

* which layer it can affect

* which parameters it can move

* which masks it can touch

* which generated objects it can emit

* which time window it can operate within

* which residues it can clear

* which audit traces it must leave

This makes access spatial, temporal, and operational at once.

The question is not only:

> is this agent allowed?

The better question is:

> from the human-centered zero-point, what angle of access has this agent been granted?

One agent may have a background angle.

Another may have a mask angle.

Another may have an annotation angle.

Another may have a verifier angle.

Another may have a director angle.

Another may only have a preview/debug angle.

Each angle is a bounded relationship to the shared surface.

Angular access lets agents generate in realtime without becoming global authorities.

They can act continuously, but only along the angle they have been given.

If an agent tries to rotate beyond its angle, cross into another layer, read a protected stream, affect the human layer, persist memory, export output, or alter the terms of the space, it has left delegated authority and must return to the human-centered zero-point.

The zero-point is not a bottleneck for every frame.

It is the origin from which valid access is measured.

Realtime generation happens along granted angles.

Authority remains zero-pointed to the human.

## The surface

The central object in Sketched is the **surface**.

A surface is not merely a canvas. It is a bounded shared medium where action becomes visible.

In Chalked, the surface was spatial.

In Sketched, the surface is temporal.

A surface may contain:

* a human video layer

* a blank stage

* a mock stream

* generated backgrounds

* generated foreground objects

* masks

* chroma boundaries

* annotations

* control overlays

* audit/debug traces

The surface is where activity becomes visible, but visibility does not equal ownership.

Something can appear on the surface and still be provisional.

Something can be generated and still be shakeable.

Something can be useful and still not be trusted.

Something can move in realtime and still remain bounded.

## Layers are claims

A layer is a performed claim over time.

It says:

> for this interval, under these parameters, with this provenance, render this contribution into the shared scene.

Layers are not all equal.

A human/video layer is presence-bearing. It should be protected by default.

A generated background is provisional scenery.

A generated foreground is an active visual claim.

A mask is a negotiated boundary.

An annotation is an interpretive mark.

A control layer is an interface for bounded gesture.

An audit layer is memory made visible.

Every layer should have ownership, provenance, mutability rules, and revocation rules.

The system should always be able to answer:

* Who or what created this?

* What does it depend on?

* Is it human-controlled or generated?

* Which agent-knob or human-knob affected it?

* Can it be shaken?

* What authority allowed it to enter the scene?

## Agents are live knobs

In Sketched, an agent is not primarily a person-like collaborator.

An agent is a live knob.

A knob is a bounded way of pushing parameters through time. Some knobs are literal UI controls. Some knobs are scripts. Some knobs are models. Some knobs are verifier processes. Some knobs are realtime generative agents.

They all share the same role:

> a knob exposes a constrained degree of freedom on the surface.

An agent-knob may continuously generate into the scene:

* drawing strokes

* generating backgrounds

* animating foreground objects

* adjusting masks

* changing chroma thresholds

* modulating style

* routing layers

* adding annotations

* changing scene tempo

* creating visual effects

* maintaining atmosphere

* reacting to the stream

This is not merely a proposal system.

The agent is realtime. It is active. It produces visible changes on the screen.

But it is active along a granted angle of access.

The agent does not own the whole surface.

The agent does not become the user.

The agent does not silently seize the camera, microphone, filesystem, publication path, or human layer.

The agent is a live control relation coupled to the shared scene.

It can wiggle. It can draw. It can synthesize. It can animate. It can mask. It can build.

But its generation must remain scoped, legible, shakeable, and accountable.

## Knobs are bounded gestures through time

A knob is not only a UI element.

A knob is a visible, bounded way to move a parameter through time.

A knob may alter:

* position

* scale

* opacity

* chroma threshold

* mask strength

* layer depth

* scene intensity

* temporal drift

* generation seed

* style pressure

* effect strength

* verifier pressure

* narrative pacing

The point of a knob is not maximum freedom.

The point is constrained expressivity.

A good knob makes a transformation legible. It gives the user an embodied sense of what is changing, how far it can change, and whether the change can be undone.

This applies to human knobs and agent knobs.

A human knob expresses authority.

An agent knob expresses bounded generation.

They are not equivalent, but they share the same grammar: controlled pressure through time.

## The two-knob principle: coordination is the security

Think about how a classic two-knob drawing toy actually works.

There are two knobs. Each knob moves the stylus along one axis only. One knob draws horizontal; the other draws vertical. A single knob, turned alone, can only ever produce a straight line along its own axis. To mark genuinely new space — a diagonal, a curve, a shape — you must turn **both knobs together**, in coordination. The stylus never lifts. Everything is one continuous, accountable trace. And when you want to start over, you shake.

This is the security model, hiding in a toy.

Each agent-knob occupies a space and may turn in a direction — its granted angle, its one axis. Along that axis it may move freely and in realtime. But **no single knob can mark new space by itself.** Novel content — a new layer, a new mark, a composition that did not exist — requires more than one knob turning together.

The human is one of those knobs.

So the safety property is not "the human approves every frame." It is structural: **new space cannot appear unless coupled knobs turn together, and the human's knob is a required factor in the coupling.** Humans turning the knobs together — with each other and with agents — is the security feature. An agent alone slides along its axis; it takes coordination, including human hands, to draw something new into the world.

Architecturally this is layers faded in and out along their axes, coupled into gestures, with **shake opening a fresh context window** for the stream to build from — a new take, based on whatever the surface now is. The trace is the timeline. The coupling is the authorization. The shake is the reset.

The concrete build of this — knobs, coupling, channels, angles, and gesture-scoped shake — lives in [`docs/11_CONTROL_SURFACE.md`](docs/11_CONTROL_SURFACE.md).

## Realtime generation happens inside delegated authority

Sketched should not require frame-by-frame human approval.

Realtime generation needs flow.

But realtime flow is not unbounded agency.

A channel is a bounded expression of delegated authority.

It says:

> from the human-held center, this agent-knob may act within this scope, on this layer, for this duration, under these constraints.

Once that channel exists, the agent may generate continuously inside it.

If it wants to exceed the channel, it must return to the authority center.

The important distinction is:

> realtime generation happens inside delegated authority; authority itself remains human-centered.

## Permission records

A lease, grant, or permission record is not the source of authority.

It is a record of authority being delegated.

It makes the delegation explicit enough to inspect, audit, revoke, or shake.

A permission record may say:

> Agent A may generate animated background texture on Layer 3 for this scene, using only the mock stream and public scene state, with no persistence, no publication, and full shakeability.

Or:

> Agent B may adjust the mask threshold between 0.2 and 0.7, but may not alter the human/video layer directly.

Or:

> Agent C may draw foreground spark effects around detected motion during this shot, but all emitted objects must be labeled generated and cleared by shake.

The record is useful because it lets realtime generation happen without pretending agents are sovereign.

Agents do not need to ask before every frame.

They do need to remain within delegated authority.

## The shared AI/user space

Sketched is not only an app surface.

It is a shared AI/user space created by a local contract. That contract lives in [`EULA.md`](EULA.md): fixed ethical clauses we control, plus a performative slot an agent drafts into during the sprint (humans hold final edit) — the way the Chalked EULA was made.

The EULA, session rules, runtime permissions, and visible controls define what kind of room the user has entered.

They answer:

* what agents may observe

* what agents may generate

* what layers agents may touch

* what can be persisted

* what can be exported

* what can be shaken

* what remains human-controlled

* what counts as authorized realtime behavior

The shared space is therefore not accidental.

It is constituted by the contract.

Sketched should make that contract visible enough that the user can understand the kind of room they are standing in.

## Human authority is not frame-by-frame micromanagement

Human authority does not mean the human must approve every generated pixel.

That would make realtime play impossible.

Human authority means the human controls the terms of the space:

* which agents are present

* which angles are granted

* which channels are open

* which layers are protected

* which outputs are allowed

* which residues can be shaken

* which memories persist

* which exports occur

* when the whole thing stops

The human holds the center by governing the space, not by manually reviewing every frame.

## The human layer is special

Sketched may eventually work with webcams, rear cameras, screen streams, 360 cameras, treadmills, mixed reality rooms, self-directed movies, generated avatars, and live compositional environments.

But MVP-0 begins with a simpler invariant:

**The human-controlled layer is not agent-owned.**

Even when the human layer is only a placeholder, it represents the part of the surface agents do not get to silently possess.

Agents may build around the person.

Agents may mask with permission.

Agents may react to motion.

Agents may annotate.

Agents may generate scene layers.

They may not silently replace, impersonate, record, publish, or seize control.

The human layer is the anchor that keeps Sketched from becoming just another generation pipeline.

## The knob does not own the surface

The strongest safety rule is simple:

> the knob does not own the surface.

No matter how intelligent an agent appears, it remains a control relation inside the system.

It is not the stage.

It is not the stream.

It is not the human.

It is not the audit log.

It is not the authority model.

It is a bounded pressure applied to a bounded parameter over time.

This is why Sketched can be playful without becoming careless.

The toy teaches the governance model.

## Masks are negotiated boundaries

A mask is a boundary between what belongs to one layer and what belongs to another.

In ordinary video tooling, a mask may be treated as a technical artifact.

In Sketched, a mask is also a governance object.

A mask decides:

* what is foreground

* what is background

* what is protected

* what can be replaced

* what can be stylized

* what can be ignored

* what remains present

This is why chroma/bluescreening belongs inside the consent architecture, not outside it.

A bad mask can erase the wrong thing.

A careless mask can make the human disappear.

A good mask is auditable, adjustable, and shakeable.

## Director layer

A director layer is optional but useful.

A director layer does not have to mean “the agent is only proposing.”

It can mean:

> a realtime authority router that allocates which agent-knobs are currently coupled to which layers and angles.

The director may:

* open or close channels

* grant or narrow angles

* throttle agent-knobs

* route agents to layers

* mute unsafe outputs

* pause generation

* trigger shake

* request user confirmation for authority expansion

* arbitrate conflicts between agents

* preserve the human layer

The director is not the soul of the system.

It is a timing and routing layer for authority.

Sketched can run with no director, one director, many directors, or a human-operated director panel.

The invariant is simpler:

> no agent gets unbounded authority over the shared surface.

## Shake is revocation

Sketched uses **shake** as its clearing gesture.

Shake is not destruction for its own sake.

Shake is scoped revocation.

A shake may clear:

* all generated layers

* one agent-knob’s contributions

* one layer

* all changes after a timestamp

* unsafe outputs

* stale masks

* failed generated artifacts

* temporary annotations

* expired permission records

* accumulated residue

By default, shake should preserve human presence.

The human/video layer is not residue.

Generated scenery is residue.

Agent emissions are residue.

Temporary masks are residue.

A shake says:

> this no longer has standing on the surface.

The system should treat shake as a first-class operation, not a cosmetic reset button.

## Shake has scope

Shake should scale to the unit of work.

In a simple playground, the unit may be a scene.

In a self-directed movie, the unit may be a shot.

In a longer composition, the unit may be a take, sequence, layer, agent, project, or time interval.

The same gesture can therefore appear at different scopes:

* shake scene

* shake shot

* shake take

* shake layer

* shake agent

* shake mask

* shake residue

* shake from timestamp

* shake generated only

* shake all except human layer

The point is not to prescribe one hierarchy.

The point is to make revocation composable.

A user making a toy scene should not need film grammar.

A user making a self-directed movie should be able to clear one shot without destroying the whole scene library.

A user experimenting with agents should be able to shake one agent’s emissions without erasing the shared surface.

Shake is the grammar of reversible authority.

## Generation is provisional

Generated imagery in Sketched is not truth.

It is scenery, hypothesis, performance, atmosphere, or effect.

A generated layer may be beautiful, useful, funny, or clarifying. It may also be wrong, excessive, manipulative, or stale.

Therefore generated layers should be:

* labeled

* owned

* parameterized

* auditable

* reversible

* shakeable

Sketched should avoid pretending that generated content has more authority than it does.

The scene is not real because a model rendered it.

The scene becomes useful when a human can see it, understand it, steer it, and revoke it.

## The audit log is memory under constraint

The audit log is not merely debugging output.

It is the memory of the surface.

It records how the scene became what it became.

Every meaningful mutation should leave a trace:

* channel opened

* channel narrowed

* angle granted

* angle exceeded

* permission recorded

* permission expired

* agent-knob activated

* agent-knob throttled

* agent-knob muted

* knob motion applied

* layer created

* layer changed

* consent requested

* consent granted

* authority expansion denied

* shake performed

* mask updated

* generated artifact added

* generated artifact cleared

Without the audit log, the scene becomes dreamlike and unaccountable.

With the audit log, the toy becomes a tool.

## Localhost is the shared center

Sketched begins local.

Localhost matters because it keeps the first version honest.

No hidden cloud.

No surprise deployment.

No silent publishing.

No background agent process pretending to be infrastructure.

No API keys required to understand the architecture.

The first shared center is the machine in front of the user.

Future versions may add collaboration, remote agents, generated media services, or live streaming integrations. But those should be built outward from the local consent model, not bolted on as exceptions.

## Visual language

Sketched uses three public metaphors:

* sketch

* shake

* knob

Everything else should be implied rather than overexplained.

The visual grammar may draw from classic drawing toys and modular synthesis, but Sketched should not copy the identity, logo, trade dress, or exact layout of any specific toy or instrument maker.

The public phrase is:

> modular-synth-inspired control surface

The core image is:

> a realtime scene with a control wall around it.

The scene is the surface.

The controls are knobs.

Agents are knobs.

A patch cable is a dependency or routing path.

A jack is a consent boundary.

A meter is an audit signal.

A shake clears generated residue.

The point is not nostalgia.

The point is legibility.

A knob makes agency visible as bounded control.

A patch makes dependency visible as routing.

A meter makes state visible as signal.

A shake makes revocation visible as gesture.

## Why this exists

Sketched exists because realtime generative media needs better primitives than “prompt and pray.”

It needs surfaces.

It needs layers.

It needs knobs.

It needs masks.

It needs angular access.

It needs revocation.

It needs provenance.

It needs consent.

It needs a way for agents to generate live without pretending they are the author of the human scene.

Sketched is an attempt to make realtime generated media more playable, legible, and governable.

Not a command center.

Not a surveillance system.

Not an autonomous content farm.

A toy-like modular surface for building scenes under constraint.

A place where agents can generate, humans can steer, and the whole run can still be shaken clean.
