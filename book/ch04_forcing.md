# Chapter 04 · Forcing: When Does a Claim Hold?

## Aim

After this chapter you can compute, by hand on a seven-condition model,
whether a condition *forces* a claim — under both of the system's
readings — and you can explain the three distinctions everything else in
this book leans on: forced vs. true-here, undecided vs. refuted, and
contingent refusal vs. torsion. You will also meet the first place the
two readings genuinely disagree, and why that disagreement is a feature
with a name (Decision Reachability), not a bug.

## Prerequisites

Chapters 01–03 (conditions, refinement, sieves, the two topologies
J_nn ⊇ J_adm and the Site Bound `lem:sitebound`). All computations here
use the **spine model**: conditions `r > {0,1} > {00,01,10,11}` (a
depth-2 binary tree), atom `a` stabilized on the left subtree
`{0,00,01}`, atom `b` stabilized on the leaves, and J_adm generated by
one admissible requirement on `r` — "leave the void."

## Definitions

**Stability, atomic** (`def:stab`, D). `Stab(q, a)`: the atom holds at q
and persists under every further refinement. The stability set
`A_a(p) = {q ⊑ p : Stab(q, a)}` is a sieve — this is where the
persistence postulate (M-Pers) is consumed, and the ledger says so.

**Forcing** (`def:force`, D). Two relations, never silently conflated:

- `p ⊩_H a` — the **calibrated** reading: `A_a(p)` is a J_adm-cover;
- `p ⊩_nn a` — the **dense** reading: `A_a(p)` is dense below p.

By the Site Bound, calibrated ⇒ dense. The converse is the whole drama.

**The clauses** (`def:clauses`, D). `∧` componentwise; `∨` when the
disjunct-forcers form a cover; and the load-bearing one:

> `p ⊩ ¬φ` iff **no** refinement of p forces φ.

Negation quantifies over the entire future. Hold that thought.

## Claims

**Persistence and consistency** (`lem:pers`, **P**, machine-verified).
Whatever p forces, every refinement of p forces; and nothing forces both
φ and ¬φ. Forcing is a promise that survives more knowledge.

**Deciding sets are order-dense** (`lem:dec`, **P**, machine-verified).
Below any condition there is always a refinement that decides φ, one way
or the other. Density in the *order* is free; whether the deciding set is
an *admissible* cover is Decision Reachability (`lem:reach`, P consuming
the smoothness contract) — chapter 05's hinge.

**Torsion is the atomic case only** (`rem:torsion`, **P** atomic /
**O** compound). "Refuted at every refinement" and "no refinement can
force it" coincide for atoms and come apart for compounds — the lab
shows the countermodel live.

## Worked example: reading the tables

Run the lab; it prints these (x = forces):

```
   ||-_nn        a    b   ~a   ~b  ~~b        ||-_adm       a    b   ~a   ~b  ~~b
      r          .    x    .    .    x           r          .    .    .    .    x
      0          x    x    .    .    x           0          x    .    .    .    x
      1          .    x    x    .    x           1          .    .    x    .    x
   leaves 00,01  x    x    .    .    x        leaves 00,01  x    x    .    .    x
   leaves 10,11  .    x    x    .    x        leaves 10,11  .    x    x    .    x
```

Five readings, in increasing order of consequence:

1. **Forced ≠ true everywhere.** `0 ⊩ a` because a is stable on 0's
   entire cone. But `r ⊮ a`: below condition 1 nothing stabilizes a, so
   the stabilizers are not dense below r. At r, the claim a is simply
   not settled — and the system says so instead of guessing.

2. **Undecided ≠ refuted.** `r ⊮ a` *and* `r ⊮ ¬a` (condition 0 below r
   forces a, so the ¬-clause fails too). r is **undecided** on a. The
   deciding set D_a is everything except r — order-dense, exactly as
   `lem:dec` promises. Decisions live down the branches; bivalence is a
   property of completed journeys, not of waypoints.

3. **Torsion.** `1 ⊩ ¬a`: no refinement of 1 will *ever* stabilize a.
   That is not "denied for now"; it is structural — a refusal stable
   under the entire future. Sketched's human-layer guard is exactly this
   shape: "consent cannot be granted" is a torsion certificate, not a
   pending request. Contingent and permanent refusal are different
   mathematical objects, and the consent layer inherits the distinction.

4. **The two readings disagree — strictness, live.** `r ⊩_nn b` (every
   condition has a leaf below it, and leaves stabilize b) but
   `r ⊮_adm b`: the leaf sieve is dense yet is *not* a J_adm-cover of r,
   because our austere generator never authorized it. Density says the
   evidence is out there; the calibrated reading asks whether *admissible
   moves can reach it*. The gap between the columns is Decision
   Reachability made visible — and note how much more the calibrated
   table withholds: even `0 ⊮_adm b`.

5. **Compound negation misbehaves.** `r ⊩ ¬¬b` by the clause — nobody
   can ever force ¬b, since every condition has a b-stabilizing leaf
   below it. But "¬b is classically stable everywhere below r" is
   *false* — at every leaf, b holds. For the compound ¬b, "no one can
   force it" and "it fails stably everywhere" diverge. For atoms they
   coincide (`rem:torsion`); the paper marks the compound case **O**,
   and the finite model checker found this exact countermodel
   (φ = ¬b at r) during the audit that produced v15.

## Exercises

1. **Predict, then run.** Add atom `c` stabilized only at `{01}` to a
   copy of the lab. Which conditions force c under ⊩_nn? Which force ¬c?
   Write the eight-row column first; then run.
2. From the table: which conditions decide *both* a and b under ⊩_nn?
   What is special about them? (Chapter 05 will name it.)
3. Show by hand that `D_a` restricted below r is a sieve (you need
   persistence — say where).
4. Enlarge the J_adm generators by one sieve so that `r ⊩_adm b`
   becomes true, without violating the Site Bound. What did you just
   "spend" to buy that decision? (This is the calibration queue in
   miniature.)
5. (Harder.) Construct a formula and a condition where ⊩_adm and ⊩_nn
   agree at every *leaf* but disagree at an interior node. Why can this
   never happen at leaves in a finite model?

## Boundary note

This chapter does **not** claim that Sketched's consent pipeline
*implements* these clauses — that is the calibration queue
(`asm:calibration`), and it is open. What is claimed: the clauses are
machine-verified mathematics (`lem:pers`, `lem:dec`, truth lemma in
chapter 05), the model is executable, and the analogy is tracked
operator-by-operator in `docs/13_OPERATOR_MAP.md` with realized/target
statuses the build re-checks. Interpretation is free; conflation is not.
